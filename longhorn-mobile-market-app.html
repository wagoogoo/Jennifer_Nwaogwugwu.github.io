Longhorn Mobile Market

Mobile App

The Longhorn Mobile Market is a mobile application built to enhance the peer-to-peer (P2P) buying, selling, and trading experience for students at the University of Texas at Austin.

1. Problem

Currently, a Facebook group page called “UT buy/sell/trade” exists. University of Texas at Austin students and alumni use this page to buy, sell, trade, and give away various items such as clothing, furniture, and even cars. However, users, namely our fellow students, voiced concerns about the limitations of searching for specific items and specific sales.

2. Solution

We created The Longhorn Mobile Market to enhance the peer-to-peer (P2P) buying, selling, and trading experience for students at the University of Texas at Austin.

3. User Research and Interviews

3.1 Interviews and Surveys

Since the UT Buy page is exclusive to UT students, we set out to find students on campus. Our interview pool consisted of UT undergrad students, graduate students, and recent UT grads. Seven individuals were interviewed, and we surveyed 26 people, 5 of whom were not aware of the system.

Interview data


We saw some reoccuring themes and statements by the interviewees. Many of the interviewees expressed that they liked the email verification feature of the Facebook page in terms of security. They also commented on the limitations of Facebook's search filters.

Interviewee 3 Quote

"I like that it's a safe place to sell, unlike Craigslist where you don't know who you're selling to or if they have other motives."

Interviewee 4 Quote

"The page should have separate tabs for buying, selling, and lost and found instead of having it all mixed up in one page. The search function should be more noticeable. I like the sub-categories that Craigslist has."


Through our research, we determined that the predominate users use the system via:

  1. Selling or Trading Posts, which sellers create and buyers interact with;
  2. Buyer’s Request Posts, which buyers post to alert users who are interested in a specific item; and
  3. Lost Posts and Found Posts, which owners and finders create.

We represented this in a usage flow model.

4. Ideation

4.1 Feature Prioritization

The results from our research suggested that UT students would benefit from a mobile app that addresses the issue of product organization and prioritizes user security.

We decided that the main organization function would be to catalogue items by different categories in terms of item type. The app would feature a verification process that made it exclusive only to UT affiliated individuals and a meetup feature to record the intended site for their transaction.

4.2 Persona and Usage Scenarios

We started by creating a persona of a typical student that would use the page. Research showed that a typical student would be a female upperclassman that lives off-campus. Many students often look for inexpensive and expendable furniture from other students who inevitably graduate and move out of Austin.

The persona data


We determined that three usage scenarios would best demonstrate the three main actions of the app. These scenarios were: a user interested in buying, a user interested in selling, and a user finds a misplaced item (lost and found).

5. Design

5.1 Interaction Design and Storyboarding

We sketched different task flows and determined the potential screens to create to follow the flow. I created a storyboard that visualized a student completing the task of posting an item on the app.

Interaction design, from the whiteboard to paper, and storyboard of a user interacting with the app

5.2 Low-Fidelity Wireframes

Sketching the wireframes allowed us to smoothly transition from paper to digital. We used Balsamiq to create our low-fidelity mockups.

Low-fidelity mockups using Balsamiq

5.3 Usability Testing

We had two phases of prototyping and testing. During the pilot testing of our initial prototype, users provided feedback to apply to our app. We used that feedback to make changes to the features.

Some issues found were confusion about the verification process, difficulty finding the meetup feature, and lack of clarity of the icons. After meeting and discussing the issues, we made revisions on the low-fis. This allowed for a faster creation of the high-fidelity mockups. Invision was used for prototyping and Sketch for creating the high-fis.

Moving Forward

Next Steps

If this app were to be developed, one iteration I would attempt is on the product detail page. If another item with an identical or similar title, description, and price were to be listed, the user can become confused and select the wrong item from the wrong person. A thumbnail image of the item on the product item screen can address that issue. The thumbnail can be similar to the image used in the message screen.

By designing Longhorn Mobile Market my group and I were able to experience the complexities of moving a system from one platform (the Facebook app) to another (a full-fledged mobile app). It was a challenging experience, but I learned a lot along the way.
